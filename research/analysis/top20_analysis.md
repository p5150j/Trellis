# Project Liberty Alliance — Top 20 Org Analysis

## Context

This document ranks 178 organizations from the Project Liberty Alliance network, scored on a weighted matrix designed for a senior tech leader (20yr startup veteran, AI/ML expert) looking to work as a fractional IC building internal tools, data pipelines, and AI/ML infrastructure for nonprofits. Based in Manila — cost is not a barrier.

### Scoring Dimensions (weighted)

| Dimension | Weight | What It Measures |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| AI/ML Relevance | 2.0x | Would their mission naturally benefit from AI/ML tools, data pipelines, or automation? |
| Tech Gap | 1.5x | Do they lack internal engineering/tech talent and need outside help? |
| Mission Impact | 1.5x | Are they doing real, measurable, impressive impact work? |
| Size Sweet Spot | 1.0x | 15-150 people — big enough to pay, small enough that one IC matters |
| Org Viability | 1.0x | Real, established org with funding and legs? |
| Growth Signal | 1.0x | Growing, expanding, recently funded, hiring? |

## Refined Top 5 — Detailed Analysis

These five organizations represent the absolute best fit for a senior AI/ML engineer looking to make hands-on-keyboard impact as a fractional IC. They are ranked not just by composite score, but by where your specific skills — building data pipelines, ML models, internal tooling, and automation — would create the most tangible, measurable value.

---

### 1. Bellingcat — The Perfect Match

**Why #1:** Bellingcat is the gold standard of open-source intelligence (OSINT) investigations. They broke stories on the MH17 shootdown, Russian war crimes in Ukraine, chemical weapons attacks in Syria, and far-right extremism worldwide. Their ~30-50 person team produces world-class investigative journalism that has led to actual criminal prosecutions at the International Criminal Court.

**Why they need YOU specifically:**

Bellingcat's entire workflow is data-intensive but largely manual. Their investigators scrape social media, geolocate images, cross-reference satellite imagery, analyze metadata, and piece together timelines from massive datasets. This is *exactly* the kind of work that AI/ML pipelines supercharge:

- **Computer vision pipelines** for satellite imagery analysis, geolocation verification, and facial recognition in open-source video
- **NLP tools** for monitoring and classifying social media posts across languages (Russian, Arabic, Ukrainian, etc.)
- **Data ingestion pipelines** to automatically collect, clean, and index open-source data from dozens of platforms
- **Entity resolution & link analysis** — connecting people, places, vehicles, and events across investigations
- **Automation of repetitive OSINT tasks** that currently eat investigator hours

They have $6.77M in revenue (2024) but their tech stack is cobbled together. They're big enough to have real problems but small enough that one senior engineer transforms their capability. They're also growing — EU grants, Swedish Postcode Lottery funding, and expanding training programs.

**Risk factors:** Based in Netherlands, may have existing technical volunteers. But the sophistication of ML work they need goes far beyond volunteer capacity.

### 2. Thorn — Highest-Impact Mission

**Why #2:** Thorn builds technology tools to defend children from sexual abuse. Founded by Ashton Kutcher and Demi Moore, they've built Safer (a tool used by platforms to detect CSAM) and Spotlight (used by law enforcement). They've helped identify over 30,000 child trafficking victims.

**Why they need YOU specifically:**

Thorn is already a tech org ($16.4M/yr, ~50-100 staff), which means they understand what engineers do and how to work with them. But their AI/ML needs are scaling faster than their team:

- **ML model development** for detecting new/unseen CSAM and grooming patterns across platforms
- **Data pipeline infrastructure** to process massive volumes of platform data in real-time
- **Classifier training and evaluation** — their detection tools need constant retraining as adversaries evolve
- **NLP models** for detecting grooming language patterns in chat
- **Infrastructure/MLOps** — scaling their existing tools to work across more platforms globally

Their tech gap score is slightly lower (3/5) because they already have engineers, but the *scale* of their ML challenge means they always need more specialized talent. The mission is about as high-impact as it gets — your code would literally help find exploited children.

**Risk factors:** They have some internal engineering capacity already, so you'd be augmenting, not building from scratch. That said, $16M/yr means they can absolutely support fractional talent.

### 3. CyberPeace Institute — Cybersecurity Meets Humanitarian AI

**Why #3:** The CyberPeace Institute analyzes cyber threats targeting NGOs, healthcare, and critical infrastructure. They tracked 3,178 cyberattacks on Ukrainian infrastructure during the war. Backed by Microsoft, Hewlett Foundation, and Craig Newmark, they're the cybersecurity arm of the humanitarian world.

**Why they need YOU specifically:**

Their ~40-80 person team works on cyber threat intelligence — a field that is fundamentally an ML problem:

- **Threat detection models** — classifying and clustering attack patterns across humanitarian sector targets
- **Anomaly detection pipelines** for monitoring NGO infrastructure in conflict zones
- **NLP for threat intelligence** — parsing dark web forums, phishing campaigns, and attack reports
- **Dashboard and visualization tools** for their CyberPeace Builders volunteer matching platform
- **Automated incident response pipelines** — triage and classify incoming threat data at scale

CHF 4.7M budget with Microsoft and Hewlett backing means institutional stability. They're Geneva-based but work globally. The intersection of cybersecurity + humanitarian work + AI is exactly where a startup ML veteran adds massive value — most cybersecurity nonprofits can't afford ML talent.

**Risk factors:** Geneva-based, may prefer on-site for some work. But their distributed volunteer model (CyberPeace Builders) shows they're comfortable with remote collaboration.

### 4. Fast Forward — Multiply Your Impact Across Dozens of Orgs

**Why #4:** Fast Forward is a tech nonprofit accelerator — they fund and support startups building tech for social impact. Their portfolio includes 100+ organizations. They raised $9.8M in 2024 and are explicitly scaling AI for nonprofits.

**Why they need YOU specifically:**

This is the force-multiplier play. Instead of helping one org, you'd help build tooling and infrastructure that serves their entire portfolio:

- **Shared AI/ML tooling** that multiple portfolio orgs can use (exactly your stated goal of "building tools they can all use")
- **Technical due diligence** on accelerator applicants' AI/ML architecture
- **Data pipeline templates and starter kits** for early-stage tech nonprofits
- **Internal evaluation tools** — ML-assisted impact measurement across the portfolio
- **Technical mentorship infrastructure** — scaling their ability to advise portfolio orgs on AI/ML

This is the most strategically interesting option because it positions you at the center of the tech-for-good ecosystem. You'd build relationships with dozens of nonprofit tech teams, not just one.

**Risk factors:** Accelerators can be more advisory/strategic than hands-on-building. You'd need to push for an engineering-focused role rather than pure mentorship.

### 5. Hopelab — Tech x Youth Mental Health

**Why #5:** Hopelab is a social innovation lab that builds tech to improve young people's health. They invest in startups, run research programs, and design interventions for anxiety, depression, and loneliness in teens. They've been doing this since 2001 with backing from the Omidyar family.

**Why they need YOU specifically:**

Hopelab sits at the intersection of technology and mental health — a field where AI/ML is both incredibly powerful and dangerously underserved:

- **Sentiment analysis and mental health NLP** — building tools that detect early warning signs in youth communication patterns
- **Recommendation engines** for matching young people with appropriate interventions
- **Data pipelines for research** — aggregating and analyzing mental health outcome data across their portfolio of investments
- **AI-assisted content moderation** for their digital health platforms
- **Impact measurement ML** — quantifying whether their interventions actually work using longitudinal data

They invest in AI startups (portfolio includes mental health tech companies) so they understand the technology. But as a research/innovation lab, their internal tooling likely lags behind the startups they fund. A senior ML engineer building their internal data infrastructure would be transformative.

**Risk factors:** Smaller budget signals. More research-oriented than product-building. But their 20+ year track record and Omidyar backing make them rock-solid.
